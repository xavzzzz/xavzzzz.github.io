    1519

    Christopher Columbus was the first European to discover cocoa in July 1502 on the small island of Guanaja (now Honduras), but he attached no importance to these "almonds".

    1528

    When Cortés returned to Spain in 1528, he brought back unknown products: tomato, white beans, potatoes, corn, peppers, tobacco and chocolate. He then tells Charles V: "A cup of this precious drink allows a man to walk a whole day without eating". But it was not until 1534 that this bitter, frothy and peppery drink attracted attention when vanilla and honey were added following a preparation prepared at Piedra Abbey.

    1615

    France discovered cocoa in 1615 with the marriage of Anne of Austria, daughter of Philip III of Spain with Louis XIII. The new queen of France quickly shared her taste for chocolate with the whole court and the clergy, it is said that the first specialists in chocolate were the monks: the clergy had a delicate taste and got better than the military in the culinary preparations .

    1659

    On May 28, 1659, Louis XIV granted by letter patent for 29 years, David Chaillou, an officer of the Queen, Toulouse "the exclusive privilege to make, sell and debit a certain composition called chocolate". David Chaillou was a street shop in the dry tree in Paris. At that time, chocolate was best known and appreciated at the court, among religious and doctors. In 1657, he entered England and in 1660 the English cultivated cocoa in their colony of Jamaica. At the same time, the Dutch as good browsers are quickly aware of the interest of cocoa beans and transport America from Europe to Europe.

    1689

    In 1689, the eminent physician and collector Hans Sloane developed a chocolate milk drink in Jamaica that was first used by apothecaries, but later sold to the Cadbury brothers. The monopoly of David Chaillou ended in 1693. The chocolate was then manufactured by a number of apothecaries and spice dealers.

    • 1700

    In the 1700s, mechanical mills were used to extract cocoa butter, which helped to create chocolate that remained hard45. It is necessary to await the arrival of the industrial revolution so that these mills are used on a larger scale. After the revolution has run out of steam, little by little, companies are promoting this new invention to sell chocolate in the forms that we know today.

    1732

    The technique of preparation was primitive and remained close to that of the Mexicans. The worker was kneeling and grinding the chocolate by hand with a cylinder on a heated sloping stone. In 1732, Dubuisson brought a first improvement by inventing a heated horizontal table in front of which the worker worked standing. Manufacturing is improving thanks to a man named Doret who invents a hydraulic machine to grind cocoa and reduce it to pulp.

    1770

    The technique of preparation was primitive and remained close to that of the Mexicans. The worker was kneeling and grinding the chocolate by hand with a cylinder on a heated sloping stone.     1780

    In Bristol, in 1780, Joseph Storrs Fry Sr. opened a chocolate paste factory: J.S.Fry & Sons. Most of its production is sold to drugstores and pharmacies in the city. In 1795, his son (Joseph Storrs II Fry) began using a steam engine to grind cocoa beans. This makes it possible to produce a large quantity of chocolate paste to make chocolate drinks, pastilles, cakes, sweets as well as medical preparations. In addition to being sold to apothecaries and pharmacists, the chocolate factory Fry supplies confectioners, managers of chocolate house and famous cooks.

    1815

    In 1815, the Dutchman Coenraad J. van Houten created a first factory followed a few years later by the Swiss Cailler, Suchard (in 1824), Kohler (in 1828), Lindt and Tobler. In 1821, Englishman Cadbury produced the first dark chocolate to eat.

    1828

    In 1828, Van Houten filed a patent for chocolate powder. 1830 sees the appearance of hazelnut chocolate invented by Kohler.

    1870

    In 1847, chocolate in tablet is marketed by the English company Fry. In 1848, Victor-Auguste Poulain created an industrial confectionery and chocolate factory in Blois.

    1870

    In 1870, Jean Tobler perfected milk chocolate. That same year Émile Menier built a modern chocolate production plant in Noisiel in Seine-et-Marne. This factory lowered the cost of chocolate in France. It is now listed as a historical monument with the adjoining working class city.

    1879

    In 1879, Rodolphe Lindt invented melting chocolate and created his first chocolate factory in Switzerland. His technique was to let the grinder containing the chocolate run for a long time to make the cocoa paste smoother. The early 1880s marked the development of the Belgian chocolate industry with the appearance of the first Côte d'Or chocolate factory.

    1920

    In the early 1920s appear the first chocolate bars. The American Mars launches the Milky Way and the Dutch Nuts his famous hazelnut bar.
